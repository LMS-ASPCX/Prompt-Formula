The Problem

There's a ton of advice out there on writing better AI prompts — some good, a lot of noise. The truth is, most weak outputs come from weak inputs. If you've ever gotten a generic or off-base response from Claude or ChatGPT, the prompt is usually why.

A 6-part prompt formula you can use for any task — writing macros, drafting emails, summarizing tickets, building Mavenoid flows, analyzing reviews, or anything else you throw at AI. We'll use real TruVolt examples throughout.

💡

Part 1 of 6
Persona
Tell the AI who it is — and it becomes that expert
Why It Works

When you assign a role, the AI activates the knowledge, vocabulary, and perspective of that role. It shifts from "generic assistant" to "specialized expert." This one change alone lifts output quality noticeably.

TruVolt Examples
  • "You are a senior TruVolt customer service agent specializing in Shop Joe pressure washers."
  • "You are a Zendesk administrator with deep knowledge of macros, triggers, and automations."
  • "You are a QA evaluator reviewing customer service interactions using the EAR scoring framework."
  • "You are a Mavenoid flow architect designing troubleshooting trees for electric outdoor tools."
💡

Be specific. "Customer service agent" is okay. "Senior TruVolt agent who handles warranty escalations for Snow Joe battery products" is much better. Specificity = better output.

Part 2 of 6
Context
The more background you give, the better the output
What to Include
  • Brand: Shop Joe, Sun Joe, Snow Joe, Ride Jetson, Bliss Hammocks
  • Channel: Email, phone, messaging, HSN
  • Customer situation: what happened, what they said, what they want
  • Internal constraints: warranty status, WTY-HOLD, replacement policy
  • Relevant product: SKU, symptom, purchase channel
Example

"A customer purchased a Sun Joe SPX3000 pressure washer from Amazon 14 months ago. It's now losing pressure and they're asking for a replacement. Our standard warranty is 2 years for this product. The ticket is open in Zendesk under the email channel."

💡

Don't assume Claude knows your setup. Paste in the ticket text, the customer's exact words, or the policy. The AI only knows what you tell it.

Part 3 of 6
Task
State exactly what you want — no ambiguity
The Rule

One clear action verb. Vague prompts produce vague outputs. Be direct about exactly what deliverable you need.

Weak vs. Strong
  • ❌ "Help me with this ticket" → too vague
  • ✅ "Write a professional email response to this customer approving their warranty claim and providing next steps."
  • ❌ "Summarize this" → vague
  • ✅ "Summarize the top 5 customer complaints from this HSN review batch into a 3-sentence executive summary."
  • ❌ "Make a macro" → vague
  • ✅ "Write a Zendesk macro for an agent responding to a Ride Jetson app retirement question. Include a subject line and a closing with next steps."
💡

One task at a time. If you need multiple things, either chain them ("First do X. Then do Y.") or make separate prompts.

Part 4 of 6
Format
Tell it how to structure the output before it writes it
Format Options
Table Bullet Points Email Numbered Steps Markdown Headers JSON Summary + Detail Scorecard
TruVolt Format Examples
  • "Format as a table with columns: Ticket ID, Issue, Agent Action, Resolution."
  • "Use markdown with headers for each section: Summary, Root Cause, Recommended Fix."
  • "Output as a QA scorecard with pass/fail and a score out of 100."
  • "Bold every change you make when proofreading."
  • "Give me a TLDR in 2 sentences, then the full breakdown below."
💡

Pro move: When dealing with a lot of data, always ask for a summary first, then the breakdown. "Give me the 3 biggest takeaways, then show me the full table."

Part 5 of 6
Examples
Show the AI what "good" looks like and it will match it
Why This Is Powerful

Examples remove guesswork. Instead of hoping the AI interprets your request correctly, you show it exactly the quality, style, and structure you expect. This is especially useful for tone-matching and format-matching.

What to Include as Examples
  • A past email response you were happy with → "Match this style."
  • A macro that worked well → "Follow this structure."
  • A QA comment from a high-scoring ticket → "Use this as the benchmark."
  • A Mavenoid node you want replicated → "Build the next section like this."
  • An existing help article → "Match this voice and format."
💡

Paste it in. Don't describe the example — paste the actual text. "Here's an example: [paste]" beats "Write it like our previous emails" every time.

Part 6 of 6
Tone
How it sounds is as important as what it says
Tone Examples
Empathetic Professional Friendly but firm Concise Confident Warm Direct Neutral / Factual
By Situation
  • Upset customer: "Empathetic, calm, solution-focused"
  • Warranty denial: "Professional, clear, not apologetic"
  • HSN caller: "Warm, patient, simple language"
  • Internal escalation: "Direct, factual, no fluff"
  • Social media reply: "Friendly, brand-appropriate, brief"
💡

Don't know the tone? Ask the AI. Type: "Give me 5 tone keywords that fit this situation." Pick the ones that feel right and add them back to your prompt.

Putting It Together
A Full TruVolt Prompt
All 6 parts in one real-world example
📋 Complete Prompt
[PERSONA] You are a senior TruVolt customer service agent specializing in Snow Joe products and warranty resolution.

[CONTEXT] A customer bought a Snow Joe 24V-X2-SB18 snow blower from Home Depot 10 months ago. They say it won't start after sitting in the garage all summer. They've already replaced the battery. Ticket is via email channel. No prior contact on file.

[TASK] Write a first-contact email response that acknowledges their issue, provides 3 troubleshooting steps to try before we process a warranty claim, and tells them how to reach us if the steps don't resolve it.

[FORMAT] Include: subject line, personalized opening, numbered troubleshooting steps, and a closing with our warranty process link placeholder [LINK].

[EXAMPLES] Use this as the tone and format model: [paste your best past response here]

[TONE] Empathetic, confident, solution-focused. Not overly apologetic.
🎯

This prompt will produce a near-ready response. You may only need to swap in the customer name and add your specific link. That's the power of all 6 parts working together.

Pro Tips
Make Every Prompt Count
Quick-reference habits to build into your workflow
Power Moves
  • Proofreading: "Bold all changes you make" — makes edits instantly visible
  • Large data sets: "Give me the summary first, then the full breakdown"
  • Unknown tone: "Give me 5 tone keywords for this situation" then pick and add them
  • Tables: Always specify column headers — don't let the AI guess
  • Iteration: Got a decent output? Say "keep the structure, make it shorter / more formal / less technical"
TruVolt-Specific Shortcuts
  • Always name the brand (Snow Joe, Sun Joe, Jetson, Bliss Hammocks) — each has different voice and policy
  • Mention the channel (email / phone / chat / HSN) — tone and format differ by channel
  • Include policy constraints upfront — "warranty is 2 years", "customer is outside warranty window"
  • Reference internal tools when relevant — "this will become a Zendesk macro" or "this is for a Mavenoid node"
🚀

Build a library. Save your best prompts. Reuse them. Swap in new context as needed. The prompt is a template — treat it like one.
